A Note on Method

How I frame the work

Most of the marketing problems I have worked on were not, fundamentally, marketing problems. They were diagnosis problems. Wrong question, wrong target, wrong data, wrong sequence. The four-step lens below is how I have learned to take a vague brief and turn it into something testable.

i.

Frame

Restate the brief as the question that, if answered, would actually move the business. "Cost per lead is high" is not a question. "Where is intent-channel-keyword mismatch driving wasted spend?" is.

ii.

Hypothesize

Pick the most plausible leverage point and write it down. A wrong hypothesis written down is worth more than a hedged one held in your head. It forces a test you can lose.

iii.

Test

Design the smallest move that proves or breaks the hypothesis. A re-segmented send. A keyword cluster rebuild. A workshop with twelve sellers. Bias toward the experiment that can run inside two weeks.

iv.

Operationalize

If it worked, turn it into a system. Lead scoring. A new workflow. A documented playbook. Pilots that do not become operating models do not compound.

Situation

I owned U.S. segment marketing for Hospitality and Multi-Unit Housing at Life Fitness, a combined ~$418M serviceable market across 50,000 contacts in three sub-segments. The leadership ask: ship the segment's first full-funnel integrated campaign.

The constraint: no agency budget allocated. Conventional path was to hire one. The alternative was to slow the work to a crawl with freelance coordination.

Hypothesis

An agency is not a building. It is a sequence of specialists challenging each other inside a brief. If I could model that sequence as LLM agents with distinct roles and perspectives, an 80/20 workflow could get to 80% of agency-grade output, then I would close the last 20% manually.

The bet was that the bottleneck was structured prompting, not model capability.

Approach

Defined ten agents, each with a role definition, perspective, and output spec mirroring real agency seats. Designed a sequenced workflow (frame » research » create » plan » challenge » refine) where each agent's output became the next agent's input.

Ran the system against the hospitality brief. Iterated. The Critic agent killed several first-draft ideas. The Strategist and Researcher together produced an audience dossier I would have paid an agency three weeks to deliver.

Outcome

Launched "A Stay That Moves You", the segment's first full-funnel integrated campaign during my tenure. The system produced the concept, brief, and four messaging pillars. Execution rolled out across social, content and SEO, web, email, and trade media around a positioning shift from product-centric to operator-centric.

Leadership flagged it as something the company hadn't executed before in the segment. The agent workflow became the default starting point for subsequent campaign work.

For FRWD

This is the GenAI-in-marketing capability FRWD's JD calls out, built and shipped in a real B2B context. When client teams ask "how do we use GenAI to accelerate marketing, not just save time?" the answer is rarely "we use ChatGPT for first drafts." It is structured agent systems that mirror how marketing work actually gets done.

Marketing Diagnostic

A 90% drop in CPL by re-framing the question.

Fuel Me · 2021–22
$900 → $90
Cost per lead, before and after.
Situation

Fuel Me's Google Ads spend was consistently producing leads at ~$900 CPL, with mid-quality output and growing friction from sales over lead fit. The default conversation in the room was about budget and bid strategy.

Hypothesis

The problem wasn't spend or bids. It was intent-channel-keyword mismatch. Too many high-funnel and informational keywords were pulling in buyers who weren't actually evaluating fuel logistics software. Reframing the keyword set around buyer intent stage, not product feature, should compress CPL and improve quality at the same time.

Approach

Audited the full keyword set and mapped each to buyer intent. Eliminated broad and informational terms. Rebuilt campaign architecture around intent clusters, not feature themes. Led the optimization sprint through an offshore execution team with a documented playbook and weekly check-ins.

Outcome

CPL fell from ~$900 to ~$90, a 90% reduction, with simultaneous lead-quality improvement per sales feedback. The reframing also fed into broader content and SEO strategy. The diagnostic mindset, not the bid optimization, was the unlock.

For FRWD

Most paid-media diagnostics start with the wrong question. "Are we spending the right amount?" almost never opens the door. "Is our targeting hypothesis correct?" almost always does. That reframing is consulting work, not media work.

Marketing Acceleration

$670K in pipeline from a single seller-led send.

Life Fitness · 2025
34 opps
From 8,000+ targeted contacts, sent from sellers' own accounts.
Situation

Life Fitness's Multi-Unit Housing segment had a warranty-related promotional opportunity. The default would have been a centralized marketing email to the database. But MUH buyers, property owners and multi-family operators, respond to seller relationships, not corporate sends.

Hypothesis

A hybrid model would beat either pure approach: marketing-built automation for scale and segmentation, delivered from inside sellers' actual accounts, so it landed as a personalized note from a real human the buyer already knew.

Approach

Built the full workflow in Pardot Engagement Studio. Partnered with three inside sellers to send from their accounts. Sourced 8,000+ targeted contacts via Salesforce + ZoomInfo enrichment. Designed segmentation logic by account type and pipeline stage to keep messaging relevant at the recipient level.

Outcome

34 new opportunities. $670K+ in attributed pipeline from a single campaign. The bigger unlock was validation: the seller-led automation pattern became reusable across the segment, and downstream campaigns built on the same model.

For FRWD

B2B acceleration isn't about scale. It's about pairing scale infrastructure (automation, data, segmentation) with relationship-led delivery. Most B2B SaaS and industrial clients have one but not the other.

Operating Model · MarTech

From one-size-fits-all to stage-aware nurture.

Life Fitness · 2024–25
50% → 13%
Hospitality CRM contacts missing job titles, before and after enrichment.
Situation

Two silent failures were undermining hospitality marketing performance. First, ~50% of CRM contacts were missing job titles, which made role-based segmentation impossible. Second, Pardot was sending the same nurture sequences regardless of funnel stage. Awareness contacts and decision-stage contacts got the same email.

Hypothesis

The tools were not the problem. The connective tissue between them was. Fix the data quality first, then layer in funnel-stage logic. Both gains would compound: better segmentation only matters if the messaging meets the stage, and stage-aware messaging only works if you can target the right roles.

Approach

Ran a data quality diagnostic and quantified the title gap. Partnered with RevOps and ZoomInfo to design and execute an enrichment run, taking missing titles from ~50% to ~13%. Then built a funnel-stage tracking system in Pardot using lead scoring, behavioral triggers, and engagement metrics, so contacts moved through stage-appropriate flows automatically.

Outcome

Role-based segmentation became possible for the first time in the segment. Stage-aware nurture replaced the one-size-fits-all sends. The funnel-stage system became foundational for subsequent campaigns, including the $670K MUH warranty case above.

For FRWD

This is the unglamorous high-leverage work most B2B clients need and few prioritize: data quality, stage logic, and the connective tissue between MarTech tools. The diagnostic muscle here is the same one FRWD's operating-model engagements call for.

Point of View · 05

What GenAI changes for B2B marketing
(and what it doesn't).

The framing I keep hearing, agencies are being replaced by AI, is the wrong frame. It points at the wrong outcome, and it lets the more interesting question go unanswered. The more interesting question is whether GenAI changes what kind of marketer wins.

My honest read, after building a ten-agent system that produced the better part of an integrated campaign: the bottleneck inside a B2B marketing team has rarely been creative output. It has been access to structured expert thinking. Strategy. Brand planning. Critical pressure on a weak idea. Most B2B marketers can write a decent brief. Most cannot afford to have a real strategist, a real brand planner, and a real contrarian in the room every time they need one.

That is what agentic systems actually unlock. Not faster copy. Not cheaper banners. Access to the seats around the table that B2B marketers have historically had to choose between or do without. The 80/20 rule applies: 80% of the value of an agency is structured argument, not finished assets.

The marketers who win the next five years will be the ones who can orchestrate that argument well. Frame the brief precisely. Pick the right agents for the question. Read their outputs critically. Close the last 20% themselves. That is a skill that looks a lot more like consulting than it looks like content marketing.

Which is part of why FRWD is interesting to me. The discipline that GenAI rewards in B2B is, increasingly, the discipline FRWD already practices.
